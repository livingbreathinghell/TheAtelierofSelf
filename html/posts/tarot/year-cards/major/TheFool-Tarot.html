֪ ׅ ֹ =͟͞‧ The Nature of The Fool ⋆་

As most people age, their egos develop and become conditioned to the normalities of society. they stop listening to their heart in fear of being made fun of, judged, laughed at, or being seen as a joke. Ones own needs and desires then become repressed and replaced with superficial concepts stemming from the need to conform.

However, the fool is released from such burdens due to his nature - which is described to be akin to that of a childs: half rational, lighthearted, optimistic. Even as he ages, he carries on the energy, beauty, and creativity from childhood and paints his reality with it.

The fool demonstrates the initiative taken to be an individual - hes the resistance to social pressures via folly. But the world isnt made for people like this - so often 'The Fool' archetype is shamed and looked down upon..

The word "fool" itself stems from the latin word 'follis', referring to objects that can be inflated with and expell air - such as bellows. This later evolved into a figurative word for an "empty headed person." But even if its "empty", a bellows constant expulsion of air increases oxygen flow towards fires - it strokes the flames into something greater, even if a bellows itself is rather prosaic.

Similarly, the folly of the Fool agitates the rigid society we've grown used to over the course of millennia. But to take on the role of the playful bellows means you're often playing with fire...

Tongue-in-cheek, the Fool pokes and prods at the scars of society and its people with playful irony and sarcasm. If it were anyone else, the lack of social etiquette could leave them reprimanded - or even dead. But no one pays a 'joke' any mind; even if what he says is laced with truth, the delivery turns away suspicion.

Soren Kierkegaard

Despite this, The Fool doesnt go through life aiming to laugh at or humilate others - instead, he wishes to laugh with them. he makes himself the butt of the joke, which dissolves not only his own social mask - but the audience’s as well. The pressure to be perfect is discarded when one willingly becomes vulnerable - it allows others to open up, and in turn act more genuine.

⋆་ The Fools Roleˎˊ

In tense atmospheres, only "a fool" would take the risk to crack a joke and lighten the mood - not just to put himself at ease, but to put the audience at ease aswell. This typically goes well, but most wouldnt dare. This innate disconnect between the Fool archetype and society gives the concept an elusive quality, making him a rare commodity.

In medieval times, the court jesters job was to entertain the aristocracy with jokes, mockery, mimicry, and jests. But often they found themselves taking on more important roles such as servants, musicians, (and for some of the more outstanding ones,) unofficial councilors and confidants. Their differing perspectives often brought attention to things that otherwise wouldve gone missed, and often their opinion influenced the kings decisions on matters.

King James VI of Scotland had a bad habit of signing official documents without reading them. His jester, George Buchanan, decided to teach him a lesson. He brought a document to the king, and as always, he signed it without a glance - just to find out 15 days later that document abdicated his rule of Scotland and made his fool, George, king instead. George had no intention of keeping power; instead, he laughed the situation off. From then on, King James always read the documents he signed.

The Fools role in an aristocracy is unlike no other, which gives them the rare opportunity to mock the monarch in their plays... and actually live to tell the tale. King Henry VIII's fool, Will Somer, is one example. In 1535, he called the kings then wife, Anne Boleyn, a 'ribald' - and then called his daughter a bastard. Henry threatened to kill the man with his own two hands before soon forgiving him. Their bond continued until Henrys dying days - it was said Will was the only person to make Henry smile despite the pain of his ulcerated leg, remaining one of the only people Henry truly trusted and confided in.

Many noblemen sought out "natural fools" (people with mental or physical disabilities). They adopted them under the guise of 'Christian charity,' when in reality they were often kept more as pets. It was believed their 'deformity' could avert the evil eye, and their raillery could redirect misfortune back to its sender.

However, sometimes this was taken more literally..

In war, The Fool fulfilled the role of psychological warfare. It was common to see the jesters of opposing leaders ride to the front of their faction and tell jokes to raise their armys morale, or taunt the enemy to provoke them into a premature attack. At night, theyd entertain the soldiers with songs and stories to help ease their minds.

Theyd often act as messengers for their leader aswell. Theyd convey messages to the opposing team - although dangerous, the role of the messenger was deemed as sacred and therefore generally they were left unharmed.

Although sometimes The Fool often joined the battle themselves: William the Conquerors jester, Taillefer, initiated the battle of Hastings himself. It's said Taillefer was so bored he rode in front of the enemy and began juggling his sword while singing derogatory poetry aimed at the opposing king. Believing he was harmless, a soldier stepped forward only for Taillefer to immediately strike, decapitate him, and charge the enemy lines. Alone, he started reciting the song 'Le Chanson de Roland' as he killed several men before getting engulfed and dying.

Many say he continued to recite the poem with glee until his last breath.

Assassins ⋆˚࿔

Although even off the battlefield, the fool still has a reputation for carrying out darker tasks. Thomas Skelton for example, the last fool of the Pennington Family.

Helwise, the unmarried daughter of Sir Alan Pennington, favored a carpenter's son named Dick over her noble suitors. One suitor, a local knight, was so enraged he hired Skelton to behead Dick.. Which he happily did as he believed the boy stole money from him. He later joked, "I have hid Dicks head under a heap of shavings.. And he will not find that so easily when he awakes as he did my shillings."

Applied Meanings |ೃ༄ ▼

The Fool represents both new beginnings and endings. Its the bridging moment from one situation to another; He is pure potential and opportunity - unconditioned, unburdened, perpetually curious.

֪ ׅ ֹ =͟͞‧ Imagery ⋆་

The imagery shows a youthful man wandering along the cliffside. Hes gazing up at the golden sky as the wind blows in his hair.

In his left hand, he loosely holds up a bindle. In his right, a white flower. Hes so enthralled he doesnt seem to notice the cliff edge ahead of him. Nature itself seems to be warning him: the wind blows against him, his dog jumps up at barks to alert him - yet it doesnt faze him.

He moves forward and focuses on the bright sides of his experience despite the potential of a downfall approaching with each step taken. He represents possibility and the duality of such - to follow your heart, but remain grounded - to listen to your instincts and the universe when it tries to guide you.

Despite representing new beginnings, the fool obviously bears the marks of his past experiences (his belongings and dog), so a journey must have taken place for him to get so far in the first place. The fools journey always repeats - after its climax it circles back around, and continues. The mountains in the background can be assumed to be the lead up to his current position - the previous journey that took place prior

Despite this, he still has a white flower - which represents purity, innocence, and rebirth. He flaunts this proudly, showing a unified and shameless sense of identity. theres no mask to The Fool - what you see is what you get. His inner motivations fuels his outer presentation.

The mountain in the background mirrors the current cliff The Fool is on - it dips where it dips, rises where it rises - until the edge.. where instead of dipping like the edge, it flattens out and continues. The pattern breaks, and its up to The Fool to dictate how it continues.

To me the mountains represents the previous cycles of this journey and acts as a reference to what comes ahead. It could be equated to ones intuition/the universe guiding you - or everything in the past thats influenced the present moment. The cliffs edge is the moment one has to put it to use and takes a leap of faith.

If the continuation of the path now depends on The Fool, and it shows the next step taken is literally a *flat* progression - somehow he must get across this edge in a *straight line.*

The only “tools” available to do this is what he already carries. The knapsack represents retained knowledge, and the staff, a straight and stable object, now becomes a potential bridge to bring the prophecy to fruition.

For him to use a stick to get past this, there must be something beyond the visible edge - another side not yet seen. The imagery represents the pivotal moment before a potential pitfall - how one acts in that moment is essential. Failure to take a moment and observe the situation for what it is may result in one falling off the edge. Even if it seems daunting, all you need to conquer it is what you have at your disposal.

Regardless, whether The Fool uses his stick to get across the gap or crawl up from the depths of it, it prompts the fool into finding ingenious, innovative, solutions.

In the imagery the sun is rising, signifying new beginnings and hope. The day is fresh, the energy is strong, and the fool is so high up hes practically one in the sky. It shows the beauty of the natural state of being - which seeps into The Fool. Free from social normalities and restrictions, the fool is in his element.

Rising suns spiritually represent the illumination of the mind after a period of darkness - which may allude to the upcoming awakening about to take place within the fool.. whether its from pure ingenious decisions, or the fall from his high.

The Fool’s dog stands back and barks to warn him. Despite its loyalty, its path ends sooner than the Fool’s — and it does not intend to follow him over the edge. Instead, the dog halts in place and stands up on his back legs as a final attempt to catch his attention.

This signifies the need to not close oneself off from their support system - whether it be spirituality, intuition, or your friends and family. These things can only take you so far - once you choose to abandon it, you cant expect it to fall with you.

Numerology ⋆˚࿔

The Fool marks the initial energy of the 'tarot journey', and consequentially is the "0" card in the deck - not quite the beginning, but not quite the end either. it represents a lack of tangible form: pure potential and energy ready to be shaped into an experience.

The number "0" in numerology is ruled by the planet Pluto and the element Ether. Its said to be the rawest and purest form of energy - it holds the potential for what comes next and is highly transformative because of such.

However, its power is said to weaken the numbers its with because "0"is so limitless. Opposing energies with structure and formation are the best match for 'The Fool', such as earth elements or king cards. (the numbers 4,5, and 8 are considered to be stable enough to manage "0" aswell.)
