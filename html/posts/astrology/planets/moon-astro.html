Essential Nature of the Moon ▼

The Moon is one of the two luminaries in astrology (alongside the Sun). She is the closest celestial body to us, and by extension, the fastest moving, completing her cycle in roughly 29 days and spending only about three days in each zodiac sign. Shes constantly in a state of fluctuation, yet always returns to the same exact places shes once been.

The moons position in the sky and the amount of light it reflects continuously shifts depending on the configuration between the Sun, Moon, and Earth. Thus, the Moon reveals where we experience fluctuation in life: where things grow and decline, where itll wax and wane, where itll appear and disappear - It reflects our earthly rhythms and cycles, showing how we, as souls, exist within the confines of a human body.

The moon is often related to our inner world: instincts, intuition, the psyche, emotions - but most overlook how it also embodies the confines in which that exists - literally how our life was conceived, sustained, nurtured - and byproduct, our human needs and body (health, physical appearance, ethnicity, sex, emotional needs, etc...)

The Moon has her joy in the 3h (which is known as 'the place of goddess.') The 3h falls below the horizon, in the night sect, which provides the moon her ideal environment. The 3hs significations embodies all the moon may desire: short journeys (especially routines and cycles), community and gatherings, siblings, the mind, communication, and the practice of one’s faith.

The Moon reflects the planets she aspects, drawing out and expressing their energies. She brings these influences into physical manifestations, which is why a "void-of-course" moon (aka, making no major aspects,) isnt ideal.

When the Moon forms a separating aspect to a planet, it represents things that have already passed or experiences from the past/early life. when the Moon forms an applying aspect to a planet, it represents things that develop in the future/later on in life.

Because of its associations with conception, the moon is commonly associated with the mother. From conception to birth, the child exists as a part of her - their bodies are one, the baby is an extension of her being. Even after birth, the native is entirely dependent: they need their mother to take care of them, feed them, and protect them..

In a chart, the Moon can describe how the natives mother shows up in their life, the conditions surrounding the pregnancy, and even early childhood: how the native was (or was not) nurtured. It represents a whimsical age and the beginning of growth - These themes branch out to include: pregnant women, maternal aunts, midwives, nurses (especially wet nurses), older sisters- even the fertility of women - the feminine archetype in general.

The Moons primary function is to absorb, reflect, and transmit. Thus, she governs the non-rational mind - the subconscious (while mercury rules the rational mind, the conscious.) The moon represents our natural responses and experiences: unconscious assumptions, inherited tendencies, parental conditioning, learned behaviors, and the mental patterns formed in childhood.

Its also cognitive processes such as intuition, emotion, dreams, and creativity, - those that operate outside of strict logic or objective reasoning. In a natal chart, The condition of the Moon is used to assess the novelty of the mind: the wits and sense (or lack thereof) and the overall mental/emotional state of the native. The aspects she makes will reveal what exactly is being absorbed, reflected, and transmitted.

No matter where she travels in the sky, the Moon always returns to the places she once was, moving in cycles again and again. In the same way, she rules over our memories and nostalgia. Its the act of returning to childhood, past emotional states, and past beginnings. The Moons nature can make this sentimental, (or if poorly placed in a chart) an individual may have a hard time letting go to the past and harbor resentment, grudges, or lingering emotions...

Even things like the household, possessions, gains, expenditure and overall fortune are ruled by the Moon. These are all things that speak directly to the natives security and mortal confines and must be nurtured and maintained. The most important lot in astrology, the Lot of Fortune (derived from the moon) speaks to this directly, showing what the foundations and conditions of life will be like for the native.

Science ▼

Among the celestial bodies, the Sun and Moon are the most directly observable in their physical effects on Earth. The Moon’s gravitational influence and light cycles have well-documented impacts on the natural world, affecting everything from coral reproduction to human sleep patterns

1. The Tides ▼

Even with its distance, the Moons gravitational pull plays the biggest role in the formation of our tides.

Tides - NASA

the Moon’s gravity tugs at the Earth, causing its mass to shift. It becomes elongated at the equator and shortened at the poles, often described as "distorting its shape ever so slightly into that of a football." The Moon’s gravitational force acts upon the entire planet and pulls on every point across its equator. As a result, the oceans bulge out on the side closest to the Moon and the side farthest from it - these bulges are what create high tides.

Less commonly known is the Suns role in this process. Twice a month, the Earth, Sun, and Moon align, causing their gravitational force to combine. This results in exceptionally high tides known as spring tides, as well as very low tides where the water has been displaced. About a week later, the Sun and Moon form a right angle relative to the Earth. the Sun’s gravitational pull then works against the Moon’s gravitational tug and partially cancels it out, producing more moderate tides known as neap tides.

credits: Vishnu Viswanathan, Joseph Renaud, NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center

2. Marine Life Spawning ▼

One of the clearest biological synchronizations with the lunar cycle is marine life spawning. This includes (but is not limited to) sea turtles, coral reefs, horseshoe crabs, sea urchins, mollusks, etc..

Sea turtle hatchlings rely on the moonlight to guide them to the ocean. They typically emerge at night to avoid predators and heat, moving toward the brightest horizon - which is traditionally the moon's reflection on the ocean

The effect of artificial light at night on sea turtle hatchling early dispersal: A systematic review of methods, impacts and findings - sciencedirect

Artificial Light at Night (known as 'ALAN') disrupts hatching turtles return to the ocean. Its their instinct to follow the brightest horizon, which is typically the moons reflection on the sea - but ALAN can confuse them and lead them to the city instead. This initial confusion has been found to cause energetic, behavioural, and physiological problems in the hatchlings - the longer they crawl around the beach trying to find their way, the more likely they are to become exhausted, suffer from desiccation, and be at a higher risk to predators. The artificial light types, intensity, and their distance to the light source are all factors in how intense the disruption may be.

credits: Daniel F. Gomez Isaza a, Ross Jones a, Phillipa Wilson b, Kellie Pendoley c, Sabrina Fossette d e, Michele Thums a

The lunar cycle also synchronizes coral spawning, allowing billions of corals to spawn at the same time and maximizing the chances for reproduction. Although things such as water temperature, tides, and sunset plays a helping role.

Synchronous coral spawning is regulated at three different points: month, day, and hour. Spawning month is regulated by the timing of gamete maturation, which is largely influenced by seawater temperature (Annual variation of seawater temperature and solar insolation may also influence spawning month.) Spawning day is determined by environmental factors that are controlled by the lunar cycle, such as moonlight and tidal fluctuations, as spawning generally occurs after the full moon. Its supported by evidence that corals (in genus Acropora) do not spawn without moonlight and that night-light pollution disturbs the synchronicity.

What is coral spawning? - National Ocean Service

On cues from the lunar cycle (and the water temperature,) hard (stony) corals reproduce by releasing their eggs and sperm (gametes) at the same time. This is crucial as most gametes are only viable for few hours - so synchronization is a must for success. This spawning cycle isnt even fully understood by scientists, who have dubbed it as one of "nature’s most spectacular events."

Moonrise timing is key for synchronized spawning in coral Dipsastraea speciosa - Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences

a 2021 study explores the nuances in Lunar-synced spawning behavior. Corals were observed to fall into 1 of 2 groups depending on their spawning pattern - one group regularly spawning several days after the full moon, while the other shows more irregularity (albeit still occurring around the full moon.)

More than 80% of scleractinian corals only spawn a few nights per year, specifically 3-7 days after the full moon, depending on water temperatures. In controlled experiments, coral colonies were covered with transparent plastic bags or aluminum foil bags that blocked moonlight at different intervals. In the natural populations (the constant factor, with no coverings), spawning occurred on the sixth night after the full moon. When moonlight was blocked, the spawning days shifted - demonstrating that lunar light plays a direct role in the timing of their reproduction.

credits: Che-Hung Lin, Shunichi Takahashi, Aziz J. Mulla, Yoko Nozawa

Hundreds of thousands of horseshoe crabs migrate up the shoreline along the Atlantic coast to mate and lay their eggs during full and new moon nights, particularly in May and June.

Lunar Cycles - The horseshoe crab - horseshoecrab.org

Prime Horseshoe spawning times occur during evening high tides at the full moon (and new moon) as they have the ability to sense light & dark cycles, detecting the Moons ultraviolet light - allowing them to follow the same spawning cycles.

Spying on Horseshoe Crabs - The Medium

Horseshoe crabs are incredibly sensitive to light, allowing them to detect the current lunar cycle and align with it. Peak spawning occurs around high tides associated with the full and new moon - this allows the horseshoe crabs to lay their eggs farther up on the beach than they usually would - protecting them from natural predators.

credits: Alex Polise

Similarly, Sea urchins often synchronize spawning with new or full moons to maximize fertilization success by ensuring gametes release simultaneously. Temperature cues dictates the timing of mass releases, and most species spawn during summer months in sync with high tides.

Lunar synchronization of the monthly reproductive rhythm in the sea urchin Centrostephanus coronatus Verrill

a 1975 study at Santa Catalina Island, California, found the reproductive rhythm of sea urchins was closely synchronized with lunar phases and not with the monthly tidal cycles; spawning occurred near the third lunar quarter in multiple years.

credits: Bill Kennedy, John S. Pearse

Quantitative study of the behavior of two broadcast spawners, the sea urchins Strongylocentrotus intermedius and Mesocentrotus nudus, during mass spawning events in situ - National Center for Biotechnology Information

Observations of multiple species of sea urchins revealed that while many spawn near the new moon, the rest were scattered across other phases afterwards. These findings support the idea that the lunar phases act as a primary cue while acknowledging other environmental factors determine the exact timing.

credits: Peter M Zhadan, Marina A Vaschenko, Peter A Permyakov

3. Marine life Migration & feeding patterns ▼

Similarly, Sea urchins often synchronize spawning with new or full moons to maximize fertilization success by ensuring gametes release simultaneously. Temperature cues dictates the timing of mass releases, and most species spawn during summer months in sync with high tides.

Marine Life is deeply influenced by the tides. Creatures that inhabit the "intertidal" zone are submerged half the time and exposed to the air half. Creatures that have adapted to being underwater all the time often find themselves trapped in a tide pool that slowly heats up and runs out of air, leaving them completely exposed to predators. In regions with large tidal ranges, the currents produced by incoming and outgoing tides are strong enough to carry many animals along with it.

Beyond tides, moonlight also makes it easier for young fish to locate prey at night, while darker times provide prey with greater cover. Some species even possess a "lunar compass" that allows them to align themselves with their environment based on the moons cycles. Salmon especially show a considerable connection to the lunar cycles - Their growth hormone (thyroxine) and the timing of its release is dependant on the moons phases.

Lunar Effects on Salmon - Geophysical Institute

A 1981 study found the level of thyroxine (the hormone produced by the thyroid gland to control growth) dramatically increases near the time of new moon. This hormonal spike allows salmon to undergo a process called smoltification where fish prepare their bodies to enter salt water. Fish released from hatcheries too soon or too late after the initial smoltification process have higher mortality rate and often fail to grow properly.

The peaks of the thyroxine hormone occur within a day or two of the time of new moon. Researcher suspect that the trigger has more to do with the amount of moonlight falling on the fishes' habitat more than anything.

credits: T. Neil Davis

Moonlight Drives Ocean-Scale Mass Vertical Migration of Zooplankton during the Arctic Winter - National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration

Lunar phases have been seen to influence the vertical migration of Zooplankton. During Artic winters the moon rises above the horizon and an increase in vertical migration is spotted. Additionally, mass-scale sinking is seen to occur approximately every 29.5 days, closely aligning with the lunar cycle.

credits: Last, Kim S. Hobbs, Laura. Berge, Jørgen. Brierley, Andrew S. Cottier, Finlo

4. sleep patterns ▼

In the days leading up to the full moon, its been observed that people tend to fall asleep later and sleep for shorter durations. Several controlled studies have explored correlations between lunar phase and humans natural sleep routine. While results vary in magnitude and replication, recurring trends show that the lunar cycle may subtly influence sleep timing and duration, particularly in environments with reduced artificial light - as its believed it may simulate the same effect as moonlight.

"Evidence that the lunar cycle influences human sleep" - National Library of Medicine

In 2013, researchers conducted an experiment which resulted in the first-of-its-kind study supporting how lunar cycles affect human sleeping patterns. They analyzed volunteers sleep structure, electroencephalographic (EEG) activity (the brain’s electrical wave patterns during 'NREM' sleep) and the secretion of the hormones melatonin and cortisol.

Around the time of the full moon, participants produced less melatonin levels than normal, a 30% decrease in (EEG) activity, and the time to fall asleep increased by 5 minutes, the total EEG-assessed sleep duration was reduced by 20 minutes, and the overall quality of sleep was noted to be worse.

Neuroscientist Kristin Tessmar-Raible (who was not involved with the study) said: "What's nicest about this study is that it uses data that wasn't originally intended for this purpose, so you know there couldn't be any bias and that makes it quite convincing."

credits: Christian Cajochen, Altanay-Ekici, Mirjam Münch, Sylvia Frey, Vera Knoblauch, Anna Wirz-Justice

"Moonlight may affect sleep cycles" - Harvard Health Publishing

In 2021, researchers examined the sleep patterns across three communities in Argentina: one on the outskirts of a city: a small rural settlement with limited access to electricity. One in a remote area with no access to electric light, and 464 University of Washington students. All participants wore sleep-tracking devices for at least 1 week, and at most 2 months.

The results showed that in the lead-up to the full moon, participants took 30-80 minutes longer to fall asleep, and their overall sleep duration was reduced by 20-90 minutes. The differences were more pronounced in communities with less access to electricity. The researchers said it's possible that the full moon made people more active at night and proposed the idea artificial light might produce a similar effect.

credits: Harvard Medical School

"Effects of lunar phase on sleep in men and women in Surrey" - National Library of Medicine

In 2021, Over the course of 2 clinical trials they recorded 205 different peoples sleep patterns (91 males, 114 females, all healthy, age ranging from 20-84.) All participants slept in a windowless room and nothing substantial was seen about the subjective sleep quality - but interesting observations were recorded in the differences in REM sleep across sexes. Females total Stage 4 sleep & REM sleep were reduced close to full moon, while males increased.

credits: Ciro Della Monica, Giuseppe Atzori, Derk-Jan Dijk

"Association between lunar phase and sleep characteristics" - National Library of Medicine

a 2014 study observed 319 patients (181 males, 138 females, people with sleep-related medical conditions excluded.) 224 participants were observed during an alternate moon, 47 during full moon, and 48 during new moon. Associations between the lunar cycle sleep parameters were examined in unadjusted and adjusted models - the results showing the full moon specifically was associated with lower sleep efficiency, less deep sleep, and increased REM, even after adjustment for several covariables.

credits: Csilla Zita Turányi, Katalin Zsuzsanna Rónai, Rezső Zoller, Orsolya Véber, Mária Eszter Czira, Ákos Újszászi, Gergely László, András Szentkirályi, Andrea Dunai, Anett Lindner, Julianna Luca Szőcs, Ádám Becze, Andrea Kelemen, Zsófia Lendvai, Miklos Z Molnar, István Mucsi, Márta Novák

5. Variation in Tree Diameters ▼

While less intuitive than ocean tides, subtle gravitational fluctuations may also influence water movement within plant tissues. Findings suggest subtle gravitational variations associated with the lunisolar cycle may influence water distribution within plant tissues - producing measurable changes in the diameter of stems. Although overlapping solar and geomagnetic cycles make it difficult to isolate the moons influence entirely, the effect is believed to be substancial enough to prompt further investigation

"Tree stem diameters fluctuate with tide" - Harvard University

In 1998, researchers studied the diameter of two spruce trees growth under controlled conditions and found they undergo rhythmic fluctuations (not dependant on facts such as light, temperature and humidity.) They did find a strong resemblance to fluctuations, timing, and strength of the tides.

When comparing their data on tree stem diameters with the local gravimetric curves (which mark the strength of gravity's pull on Earth due to the positions of the moon and sun) they found a clear synchronization. When the moon's pull was weakest, the trees were 5 to 10 micrometers wider than when it was at its strongest. This continued even after cutting the section off from the rest of the tree (as long as it remained alive.) Zürcher says he suspects changes in the moon's pull may drive the water from the cytoplasm into the cell walls and then back again, causing the subtle swelling and shrinking. This suggests that the Moon has influence in the flow of water between different parts of the trees, not just the tides.

credits: Zürcher, Ernst ; Cantiani, Maria-Giulia ; Sorbetti-Guerri, Francesco ; Michel, Denis

"Tree-stem diameter fluctuates with the lunar tides and perhaps with geomagnetic activity" - National Library of Medicine

In 2010, researchers expanded on Zürchers work. They collected 16 datasets from 6 other tree species - positive relationships were revealed between the daily variations of stem diameter and the variations of the lunisolar tidal acceleration - however the daily solar rhythm overlaps meaning theres the concern that geomagnetic forces might also be involved. They couldnt isolate the Moons influences cleanly, but their results proved lunar gravity HELPS influence variation in stem diameter at the very least.

credits: Peter W Barlow, Miroslav Mikulecký Sr, Jaroslav Střeštík

"Are there tides within trees?" - National Library of Medicine

In 2001, researchers Millet and Moallem attempted to replicate Zürchers findings by studying the growth rhythms and sap flow in mandarin orange trees under controlled conditions. They observed that the sap flow shifted from a 24h cycle to a 25h cycle when maintaining the same constant light and constant tem- perature. Their findings revealed the same basic synodic lunar rhythm as Zürcher despite changes in tree species.

credits: Peter W Barlow, Miroslav Mikulecký Sr, Jaroslav Střeštík
