Science ▼

Among the celestial bodies, the Sun and Moon are the most directly measurable in their physical effects on Earth. The Moon’s gravitational and light cycles effect on the natural world is well documented, everything from coral reproduction to sleep patterns

1. The Tides ▼

Even with it as distant as it is, the Moons gravitational pull plays the biggest role in the formation of our tides.

Tides - NASA

the Moon’s gravity tugs at the Earth, causing earths mass to shift - described as "distorting its shape ever so slightly into that of a football." (elongated at the equator and shortened at the poles.) The Moon’s gravity affects the entire Earth - pulling at every point on our planet. Because of such, the oceans bulge out on the side closest to the Moon and the side farthest from it (said bulges are what create high tides.)

And whats less known - the sun also plays a role in the tides. Twice a month, the Earth, Sun, and Moon line up causing their gravitational power to combine, making exceptionally high tides (spring tides,) as well as very low tides where the water has been displaced. About a week later, the Sun and Moon are positioned at right angles to one another - the Sun’s gravitational pull works against the Moon’s gravitational tug and partially cancels it out, creating the moderate tides (neap tides.)

credits: Vishnu Viswanathan, Joseph Renaud, NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center

2. Marine Life Spawning ▼

One of the clearest biological synchronizations with the lunar cycle is marine life spawning - this includes (but isnt limited to) sea turtles, coral reefs, horseshoe crabs, sea urchins, mollusks, etc..

Sea turtle hatchlings rely on the moonlight to guide them to the ocean. Hatchlings typically emerge at night to avoid predators and heat - moving toward the brightest horizon (which is traditionally the moon's reflection on the ocean)

The effect of artificial light at night on sea turtle hatchling early dispersal: A systematic review of methods, impacts and findings - sciencedirect

Artifical Light at Night (known as 'ALAN') acts as a disruption to hatching turtles natural return to the ocean. As its their instinct to follow the moons reflection on the sea - the artifical light types, intensity, and their distance to the light source are all factors in how intense the disruption may be - they start to crawl to the city instead. This initial confusion has been found to cause energetic, behavioural, and physiological problems in the hatchlings - the longer they crawl around the beach trying to find their way, the more likely they are to become exhausted, suffer from desiccation, and be at a higher risk to predators.

credits: Daniel F. Gomez Isaza a, Ross Jones a, Phillipa Wilson b, Kellie Pendoley c, Sabrina Fossette d e, Michele Thums a

The lunar cycle ensures that billions of corals spawn at the same time, maximizing the chances for reproduction. Although things such as water temperature, tides, and sunset plays a helping role. Synchronous coral spawning is regulated at three different points: month, day, and hour. Spawning month is regulated by the timing of gamete maturation, which is largely influenced by seawater temperature (Annual variation of seawater temperature and solar insolation may also influence spawning month) Spawning day is determined by environmental factors that are controlled by the lunar cycle, such as moonlight and tidal fluctuations, as spawning generally occurs after the full moon (with a few exceptions.) Its supported by evidence that corals (in genus Acropora) do not spawn without moonlight, and that night-light pollution disturbs the synchronicity.

What is coral spawning? - National Ocean Service

On cues from the lunar cycle (and the water temperature,) hard (/stony) corals reproduce by releasing their eggs and sperm (gametes) at the same time. This is crucial as most gametes are only viable for few hours - if theyre all synced up, the chances for reproduction is higher. This spawning cycle isnt even fully understood by scientists, who have dubbed it as one of "nature’s most spectacular events."

Moonrise timing is key for synchronized spawning in coral Dipsastraea speciosa - Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences

This 2021 study explains the nuances in Lunar-synced spawning behavior. Stating Corals are separated into 2 groups depending on their spawning pattern - one group regularly spawning several days after the full moon, while the other shows more irregularity in their spawning day (albeit still occurring around the full moon.)

(among marine invertebrates,) we have the most information of reef-building corals (scleractinian). More than 80% of scleractinian corals only spawn a few nights per year during a specific lunar phase. (3-7 days after the full moon, but dependant on water temperatures - usually spawning in around spring/summer.) They examined the effect of moonlight on spawning day in coral D. speciosa during the month of predicted spawning in the field near Green Island in Taiwan. Coral colonies were covered with transparent plastic bags or aluminum foil bags that blocked moonlight. The coverings were put in place from sunset to sunrise 1-3 days before the full moon, or 1 day after the full moon. In the natural populations of D. speciosa (the constant factor, with no coverings), spawning occurred on the sixth night after the full moon. When moonlight was blocked from 3 days before the full moon, spawning occurred 1 day after the full moon. However, when moonlight was blocked from 1 day before the full moon, and 1 day after the full moon, spawning occurred on 3 and 5 days after the full moon. The results demonstrate that without the moon, the spawn days change.

credits: Che-Hung Lin, Shunichi Takahashi, Aziz J. Mulla, Yoko Nozawa

hundreds of thousands of horseshoe crabs crawl up the shoreline along the Atlantic coast to mate and lay their eggs during full and new moon nights (particularly in May and June.)

Lunar Cycles - The horseshoe crab - horseshoecrab.org

Prime Horseshoe spawning times occur during evening high tides at the full moon (and new moon) as they have the ability to sense light & dark cycles, detecting the Moons ultraviolet light - allowing them to follow the same spawning cycles.

Spying on Horseshoe Crabs - The Medium

Horseshoe crabs time their reproduction based on the new/full moon because the gravitational pull of the moon means that the tide will be at its highest, allowing the horseshoe crabs to lay their eggs farther up on the beach than they would - protecting them from natural predators.

credits: Alex Polise

Sea urchins synchronize spawning with new or full moons to maximize fertilization success by ensuring gametes release simultaneously. This, alongside temperature cues dictates the timing of mass releases - most species spawning during summer months in sync with high tides.

Lunar synchronization of the monthly reproductive rhythm in the sea urchin Centrostephanus coronatus Verrill

In April 1975, researchers studied the monthly reproductive rhythm in sea urchins (diadematid Centrostephanus coronatus Verrill) at Santa Catalina Island, California. The information was compared to a previous study done in the summer of 1969 - the results showing the reproductive rhythm was closely synchronized with lunar phases and not with the monthly tidal cycles; spawning occurred near the third lunar quarter in both years.

credits: Bill Kennedy, John S. Pearse

Quantitative study of the behavior of two broadcast spawners, the sea urchins Strongylocentrotus intermedius and Mesocentrotus nudus, during mass spawning events in situ - National Center for Biotechnology Information

Researchers observed 24 different species of sea urchins during their mass spawning period. half clustered near the new moon, while the rest are scattered across other phases after the new moon - Results supporting the lunar phases to act as a cue, but other enviromental factors determining the timing.

credits: Peter M Zhadan, Marina A Vaschenko, Peter A Permyakov

3. Marine life Migration & feeding patterns ▼

Marine Life is heavily based on the tides - creatures that live in the "intertidal" zone are underwater half the time and exposed to the air half the time. creatures that are used to being underwater all the time find themselves trapped in a tide pool that slowly heats up and runs out of air, all while completely exposed to predators. in places with huge tidal ranges, the current from a tide going in our out is strong enough to carry many animals along with it. But besides the tides, light from the moon makes it easier for young fish to spot prey at night - while in times of decreased lunar light, it makes it harder for predators to sneak up on prey. Some creatures also have a lunar compass that allows them to align themselves to their environment based on the moon.

Salmon show a considerable connection to the lunar cycles. Their growth hormone (thyroxine) and when its release spikes is dependant on the moons phases.

Lunar Effects on Salmon - Geophysical Institute

On febuary 20th 1981, scientists measured the amounts of thyroxine in young salmon, they found the level of thyroxine (the hormone produced by the thyroid gland to control growth) dramatically increases near the time of new moon. This seems to cause salmon to undergo a process called smoltification, where fish prepare their bodies to enter salt water. Fish released from hatcheries too soon or too late after the initial smoltification process have high mortality, or fail to grow properly.

The peaks of the thyroxine hormone occur within a day or two of the time of new moon - researchers suspecting that the triggr has more to do with the amount of moonlight falling on the fishes' habitat

credits: T. Neil Davis

Moonlight Drives Ocean-Scale Mass Vertical Migration of Zooplankton during the Arctic Winter - National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration

The moons phases have been seen to influence the vertical migration of Zooplankton. vertical migration takes place in winter when the moon rises above the horizon, while mass sinking of zooplankton occurs every 29.5 days in winter (aligning with the cycle of the moon.)

credits: Last, Kim S. Hobbs, Laura. Berge, Jørgen. Brierley, Andrew S. Cottier, Finlo

4. sleep patterns ▼

In the days leading up to the full moon, its been seen that people fall asleep later and sleep less. Several controlled studies have observed correlations between lunar phase and humans natural sleep routine. While results vary in magnitude and replication, recurring patterns suggest that the lunar cycle may subtly influence sleep timing and duration, particularly in environments with reduced artificial light. (as its believed it may simulate the same effect as moonlight.)

"Evidence that the lunar cycle influences human sleep" - National Library of Medicine

In 2013, the NIH conducted an experiment which resulted in the first-of-its-kind study potentially backing up how lunar cycles effect human sleeping patterns. Scientists analyzed volunteers sleep structure, electroencephalographic (EEG) activity (the brain’s electrical wave patterns during 'NREM' sleep) and secretion of the hormones melatonin and cortisol.

It was found that around full moon, the body produced less melatonin levels than normal, electroencephalogram (EEG) delta activity during NREM sleep (an indicator of deep sleep) decreased by 30%, time to fall asleep increased by 5 min, and EEG-assessed total sleep duration was reduced by 20 min. Overall subjective quality of sleep was noted to be worse.

Neuroscientist Kristin Tessmar-Raible (who was not involved with the study) said: "What's nicest about this study is that it uses data that wasn't originally intended for this purpose, so you know there couldn't be any bias and that makes it quite convincing."

credits: Christian Cajochen, Altanay-Ekici, Mirjam Münch, Sylvia Frey, Vera Knoblauch, Anna Wirz-Justice

"Moonlight may affect sleep cycles" - Harvard Health Publishing

In 2021, researchers studied people in three communities in Argentina: one on the outskirts of a city, a small rural settlement with limited access to electricity, one in a remote area with no access to electric light, and 464 University of Washington students. All participants wore sleep-tracking devices for at least 1 week, at most 2 months. The researchers compared their sleep patterns to the moon phases - statistics showed it took anywhere from 30 to 80 minutes longer to fall asleep during the lead-up to the full moon, (losing anywhere from 20 to 90 minutes of sleep on those nights.) Sleep differences were more pronounced in communities with less access to electricity. The researchers said it's possible that the full moon made people more active at night - proposing the idea artificial light might produce a similar effect.

credits: Harvard Medical School

"Effects of lunar phase on sleep in men and women in Surrey" - National Library of Medicine

In 2021, Over the course of 2 clinical trials, they recorded 205 different peoples sleep patterns (91 males, 114 females, all healthy, age ranging from 20-84.) Each participant slept in a windowless room. Nothing substancial was seen about the subjective sleep quality, but interesting observations about the differences in REM sleep were seen across sexes. Females total Stage 4 sleep and REM sleep were reduced close to full moon, while males REM increased.

credits: Ciro Della Monica, Giuseppe Atzori, Derk-Jan Dijk

"Association between lunar phase and sleep characteristics" - National Library of Medicine

in 2014, 319 patients were studied (individuals with apnea-hypopnea were excluded) 181 males, 138 females. 224 persons had their sleep study done during alternate moon, 47 during full moon, and 48 during new moon. Associations between the lunar cycle (new moon, full moon and 'alternate' moon) sleep parameters were examined in unadjusted and adjusted models - the results showing the full moon specifically was associated with lower sleep efficiency, less deep sleep, and increased REM, even after adjustment for several covariables.

credits: Csilla Zita Turányi, Katalin Zsuzsanna Rónai, Rezső Zoller, Orsolya Véber, Mária Eszter Czira, Ákos Újszászi, Gergely László, András Szentkirályi, Andrea Dunai, Anett Lindner, Julianna Luca Szőcs, Ádám Becze, Andrea Kelemen, Zsófia Lendvai, Miklos Z Molnar, István Mucsi, Márta Novák

5. Variation in Tree Diameters ▼

While less intuitive than ocean tides, subtle gravitational fluctuations may also influence water movement within plant tissues. Theres findings that suggest subtle gravitational variations associated with the lunisolar cycle may influence water distribution within plant tissues - byproduct effecting the diameter of stems. Although overlapping solar and geomagnetic cycles complicate isolating the moons effect entirely, the effect is believed to be substancial enough to prompt further investigation

"Tree stem diameters fluctuate with tide" - Harvard University

In 1998, researchers studied the diameter of two spruce trees growth under controlled conditions and found they undergo rhythmic fluctuations (not dependant on facts such as light, temperature and humidity) however, they did find a strong resemblence to fluctuations, timing, and strength of the tides. When they compared their data on tree stem diameters with the local gravimetric curves (which mark the strength of gravity's pull on Earth due to the positions of the moon and sun) they found that the curves seemed to be synchronized. When the moon's pull was weakest, the trees were 5 to 10 micrometers wider than when it was at its strongest. This continued even after cutting the section off from the rest of the tree (as long as it remained alive.) Zürcher says he suspects changes in the moon's pull may drive the water from the cytoplasm into the cell walls and then back again, causing the subtle swelling and shrinking. This suggests that the Moon has influence in the flow of water between different parts of the trees, not just the tides.

credits: Zürcher, Ernst ; Cantiani, Maria-Giulia ; Sorbetti-Guerri, Francesco ; Michel, Denis

"Tree-stem diameter fluctuates with the lunar tides and perhaps with geomagnetic activity" - National Library of Medicine

In 2010, researchers carried off Zürchers 1998 study of 'tree stem diameters flunctuating with the tides.' They collected 16 datasets from 6 other tree species - positive relationships were revealed between the daily variations of stem diameter and the variations of the lunisolar tidal acceleration - however the daily solar rhythm overlaps, geomagnetic forces mightve also be involved - meaning they couldnt isolate the Moons influences cleanly - but results proved lunar gravity could HELP influence variation in stem diameter.

credits: Peter W Barlow, Miroslav Mikulecký Sr, Jaroslav Střeštík

"Are there tides within trees?" - National Library of Medicine

In 2001, researchers Millet and Moallem attempted to recreate and prove Zürchers study. They studied the growth rhythms and sap flow in mandarin orange trees and observed that the sap flow changed its period of 24h under regular long- day conditions (14h light/10h darkness) to a 25-h period when maintained under the conditions of constant light and constant tem- perature that Zürcher used. Their findings revealed the same basic synodic lunar rhythm as Zürcher despite changes in tree species.

credits: Peter W Barlow, Miroslav Mikulecký Sr, Jaroslav Střeštík
